What is this website for?

Fix issues that you care about with money.
That's the short answer. Here's the long answer.
There is something strange about free culture. There are are a lot of free cultural works: software, encyclopedias, music, books, fonts, films. But if you ask around, most people can not explain how they could be paid to make something that is freely available and anyone can change. Many students use free culture for their education, but they don't know how to make things like this as their full-time job, when they graduate.
How do you get paid to make something that is freely available? Here's the secret: Free software is about freedom, not price.
Okay, that's a bit vague, so lets review some simple ideas that all teenagers know. It is a firm principle of the law that you are free to represent yourself in court, unless you're a teenager and then your parents get that phonecall at 3am. No one asks, "But how do lawyers get paid?" Or doctors. Or accountants. Professionals have valuable expertise and they are paid for their time and service. Your business model is to start with nothing, educate yourself to do useful or interesting things for other people, and get better at that as fast as you can, raising your hourly rate and saving money to retire early.
This is different to aristocrats. They "owns" things and charge rents on them, or own a factory and sell copies of products. That business model is to start with a load of money, build a factory, pay workers to make copies, and then sell the copies by the million to end up with a lot more money than they started with. Its risky. If its going well, your workers might invent a better factory process and quit to compete with you, so you don't want them to be educated very well. But it might not go well, and you'll have a million copies of some junk that nobody wants any more, so you don't want progress to go very fast.
Computers copy and modify information. They are factories of information to make copies — without cost. If you're an aristocrat, it is an amazing invention. You start with a load of money, pay workers to make Version 1.0, and sell copies that cost you nothing to make. That's how you get to be "Bill" Gates, selling nothing for something. But did you know that when he was born, he was already considerably richer than you. His full name is "William Henry Gates, The Third" and he is an aristrocrat. He personally invented owning software.
The flaw in this plan is that software is not like hardware, in two important ways.
First, copies are without cost, so everyone can make their own copies, for free. That means aristocrats must punish people for sharing copies of the things they own. If you live in a country where this doesn't happen, there is still an ethical issue. Remember when your friend asked you not to share something? It would be rude to post it on the web anyway. You might not be friends with Bill Gates, but it is just as uncool to share things you are asked not to share.
Second, all software has issues, so Version 1.0 needs to be updated to Version 1.1 and later it will need to be completely rewritten as Version 2.0. This means the copies need to be modified, but if Version 1.0 is still selling, why bother? If 1 million people bought copies and you and 10,000 other people care about an issue, you are just 1% and can be ignored. We have all lived in such dark places.
Freedom is the way out. Copies can be shared freely with friends, and anyone is free to modify them — if you know how. If you don't, you are free to learn everything (everything!) about how it work. So even if you are starting with nothing, you can educate yourself to do useful and interesting things for other people. The hourly rate can be pretty good.
That's the secret: Professionals get paid for our time. Instead of users paying rents for proprietary (and therefore boring) things, in our lively free culture we pay for time it takes to improve free, dynamic, exciting things.
We read and write what we like. We pay our time. And in time, we will have everything.
(Technically, Freedom Sponsors is a crowd-funding platform for giving and taking bounties on programming tasks for Free, Libre, Open Source Software. Join us.)

What makes it unique?

We run this project as close to the ideals of freedom and openness as possible. For example, the project founder Tony lives in Brasil, so if you ever come visit, he'll buy you a caipirinha!

Our strong philosophy means we put users first in everything we do. Here's a few examples:

  • We want to build a community based on trust and reputation.
    Sponsors only have to pay their bounty after the developer resolves the issue (but you can choose to pay before, if you wish.)
  • We want to build free, libre, open source software.
    Our users' software freedom is important to us, so our software is free, libre, open source on the front-end and the back end. We don't use third-party javascript code, which can allow our users' online activities to be spied on. This includes Google Analytics, Facebook Like buttons, and things like that. We do follow the FSF guidelines for free, libre javascript code.
  • We want to be the change we wish to see in the world.
    We're "open everything", so everything we do is out in the open: Check out our website statistics, browse our blog, and see the way we handle all feedback - good and bad - with no stress. Its an idea shared with our friends at Gittip, who are an "open company."

Do you really think that it will work?

Yes. Honestly, we do. We believe that there are enough decent, honest people in the world for this to work.
A wise guy once said, stay foolish.
Have you heard of the Agile Manifesto? Freedom Sponsors is all about taking the third Agile principle to its ultimate consequence: "Customer collaboration over contract negotiation."
We give regular people, who use libre software but can't write code, the ability to push forwards the projects they love. No contracts, just trust that developers also love these projects too, and feel happy when they can be paid to put more work in to them.

How can I tell if a sponsor is serious about his offer?

Like eBay, you should check out their profile and take a look at their history. If there are a lot of unpaid offers on resolved issues, that's a bad sign.

So you have a reputation system?

Yes, but later. (That's the way clever people say no.) We just display the user history attached to a profile.
But we intend to have something more sophisticated in the future.

How do I add credit to Freedom Sponsors?

Actually you just pay each developer directly.

What about privacy?

Our purpose is to do business in public. We don't show your email address, but everything that you do here (making an offer, saying you'll work on an issue, all your comments) is public for the world to see.

How do payments work?

Payments can be made either in USD via Paypal, or in Bitcoins. Before you can send payments, you’re going to need a Paypal account or a Bitcoin wallet. Before you can receive payments, set up both so you can accept all offers. We check that each Paypal account is valid before allowing anyone to make a payment to it.

Freedom Sponsors charges a 3% fee on top of offers, regardless of the payment method. We clearly state this on each payment page. To keep things simple, we use Paypal’s Parallel payment type, so a single payment is sent to two or more receivers.

For example, when you want to pay a US$10 bounty, you may decide to give US$5 each to 2 developers. On Paypal’s website, you will see:

  • Developer A: USD $5
  • Developer B: USD $5
  • Freedom Sponsors' 3% fee: USD $0.30
  • PayPal's 10% Fee: USD $1.03
  • TOTAL: USD $11.33
Bitcoin has no “parallel payment.” Instead, you send us, we verify the transaction, keep 3%, and transfer the rest to the developer(s) being paid.

Since our system is libre, you can review the code that processes transactions for both Paypal and Bitcoin.

I don't have a Paypal account or a Bitcoin wallet, is that ok?

It is okay for commenting. To send or receive payments, you’ll need to set up one of these options on your profile page.

Two developers are claiming to have resolved my issue. What should I do?

  1. Verify that the issue is really solved. We encourage you to pay only after your problem really is fixed.
  2. Decide which developer to pay, or better yet, decide to pay both of them.
  3. (Decide how much to pay each of them.)
  4. Pay them and then tweet about how you feel.

To make a fair choice, do a little digging and check out the original issue to see how it went down. Maybe one person submitted a pull request for a bugfix, and another person had to rework it and then merged it. They both deserve some thanks, and your money. You decide how much you pay to whom. The choice is yours.

An independent developer fixed my issue. How do I include it in the next release of a project?

We make the "No Forking" checkbox part of the default acceptance criteria. This means that getting solutions included in the main project is needed for them to be accepted, and it is by default because we want to encourage sponsors to actually fund the original projects.

I resolved an issue, but the sponsor just didn't pay me. What can I do?

Try to resolve conflict with compassion. An excellent book that explains how to do this is Marshall Rosenberg's "Non-Violent Communication: A Language of Life". Simply, tell them how you feel without judging them or evaluating the situation, then request a description of what their ideal situation is, and ask for suggestion about how to get there.
If you feel upset, and judge the sponsor as not trustworthy, remember that the issue - and its comment history - will be publicly linked to the sponsor's account. If you feel unhappy, we suggest you go to sleep before posting anything you might later regret.
(Sadly this can, very occasionally, happen. Normally we have an atmosphere of gratitude and collaboration among sponsors and developers.)

Can I get paid before I complete the issue?

Yes. When you start working on an issue, simply check the “start accepting payments” box, and kindly ask your patrons to pay you.
We recommend only doing this when you're confident that you will be able to complete the issue on time and on budget. If you think the total payment should be higher, track down some other users who suffer from this issue and ask them to checkout Freedom Sponsors xxx

Can I send a payment before the issue is completed?

Maybe. Normally you have to wait until the developer has marked the issue as “DONE.” You can do this when the issue is “IN_PROGRESS” and the developer has indicated that they wish to start accepting payments. But we strongly encourage you to only pay for finished issues.

I paid for an unfinished issue, and the developer won't deliver. What can I do?

Ask for a refund — from the developer. We can not refund you because payments are made directly to developers. We hope you've been polite, so far.
(This is why we encourage to only pay for finished issues. )

Does Freedom Sponsors have any kind of integration API?

Yes. Today it is basic, and we hope you'll use it and contribute to making it better. Please check our Developers section.
