What is FreedomSponsors?

FreedomSponsors is a crowdfunding platform for programming tasks of Free and Open Source Software.
You can place money bounties for issues that you care about getting fixed, and you can collect bounties by working on open issues.

What makes FreedomSponsors unique?

We have a strong philosophy based on openness and freedom, so we try to put the community first in everything we do. That philosophy also shows in how our platform works. Here's a few examples:

  • We want to build a community based on trust and reputation. So sponsors only have to pay their bounty after the developer solves the issue (but they can choose to pay before if they want too)
  • FreedomSponsors is not only open-source (front-end and back-end), we're "open-everything", having a lot in common with Gittip's "open company" concept.
    Everything we do is out in the open: You can see our site stats, we're constantly blogging about stuff, and we're wide open for users feedback.
  • We value our users software freedom.
    For instance, we don't use third party javascript code that can allow our users online activities to be tracked by those third parties - meaning Google Analytics, the Facebook like button and things like that.
    We also follow FSF's conventions for publishing free javascript code - because we believe those things are important.
And last but not least, FreedomSponsors is made in Brazil. If you ever come visit, let's have some caipirinhas together! :-)

Do you really think that idea could work?

We really do. Because we believe that the amount of decent honest people in the world is way higher then the minimum required for something like this to work.
Yeah, we'll keep on staying foolish, just like uncle Steve taught us.
Have you heard of the Agile Manifesto? FreedomSponsors is all about taking the third principle to its ultimate conseqences:

  • "Customer collaboration over contract negotiation".
We believe FreedomSponsors will give people the ability to give the right push to open source projects, so that things go faster; while also enabling programmers to do more work on the projects they love.

How can I tell if a sponsor is serious about his offer?

You can take a look at his/her history. Everything you do at FreedomSponsors is public.
Check out their profile. If there are a lot of unpaid offers on resolved issues, that's a bad sign.

So you have a reputation system?

Not yet. We just display the users history attached to their profiles.
But we intend to have something like that in the future.

How do I add credits to FreedomSponsors?

You don't.
Payments go directly to the developer you choose to pay.

What about privacy?

We don't show your email address. Everything else that you do here - offers, working, commenting - is public for the world to see.

How does payment work?

Payments can be made either in US$, via Paypal or in Bitcoins.
Before you can send or receive payments, you’re going to need one or the other.
If you’re a developer who will receive payments, it’s best that you have both a Paypal account and a Bitcoin wallet – that way, you’ll be able to receive offers in US$ and in Bitcoins.

Regardless of the payment method/currency, FreedomSponsors will charge an extra 3% fee of top of payments.
This is very much visible on the payment page.

For Paypal payments, we use Paypal’s Parallel payment type. On a parallel payment an account A can make a single payment that will be distributed to two or more receiver accounts.

Suppose a sponsor wants to pay a US$10 bounty to two developers and decides to give US$5 to each. On the payment page (on Paypal’s website) the sponsor will see:

  • Developer A ....... US$ 5.00
  • Developer B ....... US$ 5.00
  • FREEDOMSPONSORS ... US$ 0.30
And there’s also Paypal's fee on top of that.
Also, FreedomSponsors actually verifies whether a receiving Paypal account is valid before allowing a sponsor to make a payment to it.

For Bitcoin payments, the same principles apply, except that there’s no “parallel payment” – you send the bitcoins FreedomSponsors wallet.
Once the transaction is verified, FreedomSponsors will keep the 3% fee and transfer the remaining bitcoins to the developer(s) being paid.

Since FreedomSponsors backend is open source, anyone can review the code that processes transactions, both for Paypal and Bitcoin.

I don't have a Paypal account or a Bitcoin wallet, is that ok?

You’ll need one or the other before you can send or receive payments.
You can set your Paypal email and your Bitcoin wallet address in your profile page.

Two developers are claiming to have resolved my issue. How should I pay?

First you should verify whether the issue is really solved. We encourage sponsors to pay only after their problem is gone!

Then you have to decide whether you'll pay developer A, developer B, or both. To make a fair choice, do a little digging and check out the original issue to see which one(s) of them really worked on it.

For example, maybe developer A submitted a patch for a bugfix, and developer B had to do some rework on it before integrating it into trunk. In that case, they both deserve some credit.
You'll have the option to select how much you pay to whom. The choice is yours.

How a fix done by an independent developer will return back to the main project (trunk)?

Ideally, there should be no "independent developers". The best way for a developer to contribute to an open source project is to actually get involved with the corresponding community.
In most cases, it is also a bad thing for the end user (meaning, the sponsor) if the work is done on a branch, or a fork of the main codebase, because then the user would not benefit from new improvements that eventually get added in the future.

The "Sponsor issue" form has a "No Forking" checkbox as part of the acceptance criteria. By checking it, the sponsor is telling that only solutions that are implemented on the main project's codebase will be accepted.
We want to encourage sponsors to actually fund the original projects, so that checkbox comes checked by default.

I resolved an issue, but the sponsor just didn't pay me. Is there anything I can do?

Fortunately this doesn't happen very often. What we've seen so far is an atmosphere of gratitude and collaboration among sponsors and developers.
When problems like this arise, we encourage our users to use the issue comment system to try to sort it out.
That way, if a sponsor really cannot be trusted, that issue - and its comment history - will be publicly linked to the sponsor's account.

Can I pay the developer before he actually finishes the issue?

It depends.
When the developer starts working on an issue, he/she can check a flag: “start accepting payments”.
Sponsors can only make payments to issues in the “DONE” state, or issues in the “IN_PROGRESS” state that are accepting payments.

I paid for an unfinished issue, and the developer won't deliver. Is there anything I can do?

Since payments are made directly to developers, FreedomSponsors is unable to refund you in this case.
We encourage you to only pay for unfinished issues if you really trust the developer will meet your requirements.
